# 30 — Conveying Go's depth (the difficulty / appreciation arc)

**Status:** 📝 Design capture (Patrick, 2026-06-27, from the first-7yo playtest). Not scheduled.
**Priority:** Medium — a journey / retention concern, not a v1 blocker.

## The problem (observed)
First non-Roland/non-Max 7yo through the app (Roland's friend's son, 2026-06-27):
after beating the beginner bot (Seedling 30k) easily, he concluded **Go is easy.**
Only when he stepped up to a harder bot did he start to feel the depth — but with
little instruction or guidance to make sense of *why* it suddenly got hard.

The risk: a kid forms "this is a simple capture game," then either drifts off
(looks shallow) or hits the difficulty wall with no framing for it. **Go's depth
and difficulty are a big part of the appeal** — and we should get that across
*before* a player goes too far thinking it's trivial.

## Thesis
The beginner bot is *designed* beatable — that gentle on-ramp is correct, keep it.
But "I'm winning easily" must not harden into "this game is shallow." We want an
early, honest taste of the depth: enough to intrigue ("there's a whole iceberg
under this"), not enough to crush. The calibrated ladder already handles
*difficulty pacing*; this is about *appreciation* — making the depth legible and
appealing, and making the inevitable step-up feel like a guided climb rather than
a wall.

## Rough ideas (unsorted — for a design session)
- **Play of the Game as a depth-revealer (fp 28).** After an easy win, surface
  "you won — but look what was *really* happening here" (a deep move, a bigger
  point left on the board). Turns a blowout into a glimpse of the iceberg.
- **Frame the ladder as a mountain, not a checkbox.** Make the *range* visible
  early (Seedling = foothills; Void / 1d = the summit) so easy first wins read as
  base camp, not the top.
- **A "wonder" beat.** A kid-sized awe moment after the first few wins — Go has
  more positions than atoms in the universe; pros study it for life. Curiosity,
  not intimidation.
- **Guide the step-up.** When the bot ramps and it suddenly feels hard, *say why*
  ("Sprout keeps its stones connected — watch your weak groups"), so the spike
  comes with a handhold.
- **Show a taste of beauty.** A famous shape, a ladder/net "magic trick," a tiny
  pro snippet — depth as wonder, not homework.

## Connects to
- **fp 28 (Play of the Game)** — the in-context teacher is the natural vehicle for
  the depth-revealer beats.
- **fp 01 / 20 / 24 (calibrated ladder)** — difficulty *pacing* is solved; this is
  the *narrative* wrapped around it.
- **Retention thesis (fp 28 intro)** — the "wall" is honest progress; this makes
  the wall feel *appealing* (a climb worth making) rather than discouraging.

## Open questions
- *When/where* to deliver a "depth" beat without nagging a kid who's having fun?
- *How much* is enough — intrigue vs. intimidation — for a 7yo specifically?
- **Is "appreciation of depth" even the right lever for the youngest band?** The
  7yo wanted to keep playing regardless; the depth message may matter more at the
  10–12 / middle-school band (and for parents). Tie to the camp age A/B — don't
  assume the 7yo's "it's easy" is a problem to solve vs. a healthy on-ramp.
